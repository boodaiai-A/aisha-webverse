Reflection on: Against an Increasingly User-Hostile Web

I never really thought about how frustrating the internet is until this. I’m so used to ads and pop-ups that I stopped noticing them.

Now I see that a lot of websites don’t actually feel helpful. They feel like they are trying to keep me there as long as possible. I’ll open something for one reason, then end up clicking random things without meaning to.

There’s also so much happening at once notifications, moving images, ads. It feels overwhelming and makes it harder to focus.

What stayed with me is that this is intentional. That made me uncomfortable because I never thought about design like that before.

It also made me think about how the internet used to feel more simple and open. Now it feels more controlled.

After this, I think I’ll pay more attention when I’m online. Websites don’t need to feel this overwhelming.

Reflection on: Callum Copley — A Friend is Writing

This one felt a little uncomfortable, but in a good way. It made me notice things I usually ignore when I’m texting.

I kept thinking about how easy it is to misunderstand people. A short message can feel confusing, and I start overthinking even if nothing is wrong.

Waiting also matters more than I realized. When someone takes time to reply, it changes how I feel. Even silence starts to feel like something.

It made me realize how strange online communication is. We’re talking to people, but also alone at the same time. It’s not like real life.

Now I’m more aware that it’s not just the words, but also timing and silence.

Reflection on: Laurel Schwulst — My Website is a Shifting House

At first I didn’t fully understand this, but after thinking about it, I started to like it more.

I usually think a website should be finished and clean. But this reading made it feel like something that keeps changing over time.

I liked the idea of a website as a space, not just information. It can feel personal and doesn’t have to be perfect.

It also made me feel less stressed. I always feel like my work isn’t ready yet or needs fixing. This made that feel normal.

Now I see a website as something that can grow with you instead of something that has to be complete.

Reflection on: Ursula K. Le Guin — A Rant About Technology

I realized I think about “technology” in a very narrow way. I usually think about phones or computers, not simple things.

It was interesting to see that everyday objects are also technology. Things we use all the time but don’t think about.

The idea that stuck with me is that technology is just how humans interact with the world. That made it feel more normal and less complicated.

It also made me question how we use the word “technology” to mean something advanced, when that’s not always true.

Now I understand it in a broader way. It includes simple, everyday things too.

Reflection on: J.R. Carpenter — A Handmade Web

The internet now feels very clean and polished, but also very repetitive. Everything looks similar.

The idea of a “handmade web” felt more real. Like something made by a person, not just a template.

I also never thought of imperfection as something positive. I usually try to fix everything, but this reading made it feel okay to leave things a little messy.

Sometimes perfect websites feel boring. Simpler ones can feel more honest and personal.

Now I feel like my work doesn’t always have to be perfect. It just needs to feel like mine.

Reflection on: Becca Abbe — The Internet’s Back-to-the-Land Movement

I always thought of the internet as something unlimited and invisible, but now I see that it depends on real machines and energy.

It was also interesting how it talks about moving away from big platforms. A lot of the internet is controlled by companies, and we don’t always notice.

Smaller websites feel more personal and real. That connects to what we’re doing in class, building our own sites.

This reading made me realize that design isn’t just visual. It’s also about choices and control.

Now I think more about what’s behind the internet, not just what I see on the screen.
